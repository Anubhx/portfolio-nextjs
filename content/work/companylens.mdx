## Why this mattered

Due diligence is an industry of brute force. Analysts spend hours digging through SEC filings, 10-Ks, and news reports, just to pull out a few salient risks and opportunities. The work is high stakes, but the process is profoundly uncreative. 

CompanyLens mattered because it wasn't just about reading text faster—it was about orchestrating a workflow. If we could build a system that performed the initial heavy lifting of research, we could free analysts to do what humans actually do best: make judgment calls.

## What made it difficult

Retrieving text is easy. Retrieving the *right* text in the right context is incredibly hard. 

Large Language Models have a notorious habit of hallucinating when summarizing massive financial documents. A simple RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation) pipeline wasn't enough. The system needed to know when a piece of retrieved information was contradicted by another document, and it needed to synthesize hundreds of pages without losing the nuance of risk factors.

## How I approached it

I moved away from a single-prompt architecture and designed a multi-agent system using LangGraph.

1. **The Research Agent:** Responsible purely for searching the vector database (ChromaDB) and identifying relevant chunks of SEC filings.
2. **The Analysis Agent:** Takes the retrieved chunks and acts as a skeptic. It cross-references the data and looks for contradictions or missing context.
3. **The Report Agent:** Synthesizes the verified findings into a structured, executive-ready format.

This wasn't just an engineering choice—it was a UX choice. By separating the concerns, the system became significantly more reliable, and we could expose the "thinking" steps to the user, building trust in the final output.

## Trade-offs

I chose to prioritize accuracy over speed. 

Generating a full diligence report takes about 90 seconds. In the age of instant chat interfaces, 90 seconds feels like an eternity. But in the context of due diligence, 90 seconds to replace 3 hours of manual reading is a miracle. To bridge the UX gap, I implemented streaming responses so the user could watch the agents "think" and compile the report in real-time, transforming a loading state into a moment of engagement.

## Reflection

CompanyLens taught me that the UI of AI is rarely a chat box. The real interface of AI is workflow. 

Users don't want to chat with their documents; they want their documents to do work for them. Designing an intelligent system means designing the choreography of tasks, not just the prompts.
