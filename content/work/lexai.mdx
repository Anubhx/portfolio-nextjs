## Why this mattered

Legal contracts are intentionally dense. They are engineered to be legally precise, which often makes them humanly unreadable. For small business owners, freelancers, and non-legal teams, signing a contract is often an act of blind faith mixed with anxiety.

LexAI was built to bridge this asymmetry. It wasn't about replacing lawyers; it was about giving everyday people a tool to understand the risks they were taking on before they signed the dotted line.

## What made it difficult

Lawyers do not think in keywords; they think in implications. 

A standard search function can find the word "indemnity," but it cannot tell you if the indemnity clause is unusually aggressive. The technical challenge was teaching a system to read a 40-page PDF, extract the semantic meaning of clauses, and score them against standard legal baselines without hallucinatory legal advice.

## How I approached it

I built a pipeline centered around semantic extraction and risk scoring using Gemini and pgvector.

First, the system chunks the uploaded contract intelligently—not just by character count, but by legal clauses. Then, it maps these chunks into a vector database.

Instead of presenting the user with a generic chat interface, I designed a Dashboard. The dashboard immediately highlights the contract's "Risk Score" and flags specific clauses (e.g., Liability, Termination) with a traffic-light system (Red, Yellow, Green). The user can then click on a flagged clause, and the AI explains *why* it's risky in plain English, referencing the exact original text.

## Trade-offs

The UI had to be incredibly constrained. 

Initially, I considered allowing users to "negotiate" with the AI to rewrite clauses. I scrapped it. The risk of the AI generating legally binding but flawed text was too high. Instead, I constrained the system to be read-only and analytical. It highlights problems; it doesn't try to play lawyer and write solutions. This constraint made the product vastly more trustworthy.

## Reflection

LexAI reinforced my belief that the best AI products don't feel like AI products. They feel like calm, competent utilities. 

When you remove the blinking cursor of an open chatbox and replace it with a structured, opinionated interface, you relieve the user of the burden of prompt engineering. You just give them the answers they didn't know how to ask for.
