## Why this mattered

Most language learning apps are designed like video games. They optimize for daily streaks and gamified dopamine hits, rather than actual conversational fluency. Users can spend 300 days on an app and still freeze when a barista asks for their order in a foreign language.

LanguageTalk mattered because it treated language as a social utility, not a puzzle to be solved. The goal was to design an experience around the psychology of habit and natural conversational recall.

## What made it difficult

You can't fake fluency. 

The hardest part of this project was entirely human: understanding why people abandon language learning. Through 12 in-depth user interviews and diary studies, the pattern became clear. People quit because the learning didn't transfer to their real lives. The interface challenge was creating a system that felt like a safe sandbox for conversation, without feeling like a rigid classroom.

## How I approached it

I threw away the "skill tree" paradigm completely.

Instead, the information architecture was redesigned around a "conversational loop." Users are dropped into low-stakes, AI-driven scenarios (ordering a coffee, asking for directions, negotiating a price at a market). 

I built a high-fidelity prototype in Figma (over 60 screens) that mapped out the exact micro-interactions of speaking, failing, and receiving immediate, gentle corrections from the AI. The UI was deliberately stripped back—massive typography, clear audio waveforms, and zero distracting gamification badges. The focus was entirely on the voice and the text.

## Trade-offs

I sacrificed short-term engagement metrics for long-term retention potential.

Gamification works really well to get users to open an app for 3 minutes a day. By removing leaderboards and streaks, the app felt "quieter" and demanded more cognitive effort from the user. It was a risky UX trade-off, but the hypothesis was that users would stay longer if they actually felt themselves becoming fluent, rather than just chasing a high score.

## Reflection

LanguageTalk was an exercise in restraint. 

As a designer, the temptation is always to add—more colors, more animations, more reward screens. This project forced me to strip everything away until only the core utility remained. It taught me that sometimes the best user experience is simply getting out of the user's way and letting them focus.
